Gantt scheduling, daily truck-allocation math, and scenario planning for the completions team coordinating sand across multiple frac fleets — the operator-side planning layer that today lives in Excel because every incumbent's software is welded to their hardware.

The idea

Problem

A modern frac crew burns a truckload of sand every ~10 minutes around the clock; last-mile logistics is as much as a third of delivered sand cost, and a crew waiting on sand costs $5,500-$10,000+ per hour. The software incumbents all sell hardware or services first — Solaris silos, PropX containers with PropConnect, Atlas trucking with Opti dispatch — so their tools optimize their own asset utilization and dispatch, not the operator's cross-fleet plan. The operator-side questions — which pads run on which fleet lanes, how many trucks per hauler per day, what breaks if a pad slips a week, baseline vs. forecast vs. actual — live in Excel, rebuilt by every completions team, breaking silently under simul-frac pace.

Solution

FleetSync is the operator-side sand logistics command center: an interactive Gantt of frac jobs by fleet lane with drag-and-drop rescheduling and lane cascading, an Excel-like daily truck-allocation grid with hauler totals / frac needs / surplus rows and real-time conflict detection, truck-demand math from stage pace (stages/day × tons/stage → loads/day with storage-type overrides), scenario management (baseline / forecast / actual / sandbox with isolation), an NPT journal with sub-categorized causes, and CSV/Excel import-export both ways. Hardware-agnostic by design — it doesn't care whose boxes, silos, or trucks are on location.

The wedge: The sand-logistics coordinator at a gas operator running multiple frac fleets — a planning buyer, not a dispatch buyer. Win the plan (the Gantt + allocation grid that replaces the sacred spreadsheet), and expansion into actuals, analytics, and hauler-facing views follows. Every incumbent must sell hardware to justify their software; this sells the plan itself.

Why now

Gas completions activity is entering a multi-year ramp: the 2026 LNG wave (Corpus Christi Stage 3 and Plaquemines Phase 2 both due online) has the EIA projecting record US natural gas production in 2026 and 2027, and Appalachia alone is estimated to need ~50 additional rigs and frac crews to deliver the next 35 Bcf/d, with ~824 drilled-but-uncompleted wells queued. Meanwhile sand intensity keeps climbing (~2,500 lb per lateral foot is now the sweet spot in core Marcellus) and simul-frac/e-frac pace compresses the margin for logistics error. The incumbents consolidated around hardware (Liberty acquired PropX; Atlas built dispatch around its own trucking), leaving the operator-side planning layer unclaimed. And the build cost collapsed: this app itself was AI-assisted into existence by a practitioner on the job.

  • LNG pull: Corpus Christi Stage 3 and Plaquemines Phase 2 liquefaction online in 2026; ~80% growth in US LNG export capacity projected over five years.
  • EIA projects record US natural gas production in 2026 and 2027 — completions activity follows.
  • Appalachia: ~824 DUCs and an estimated ~50 additional rigs/frac crews needed for 35 Bcf/d of new production — each new crew is another coordination surface.
  • Sand intensity: ~2,500 lb/lateral-ft designs and simul-frac operations multiply daily tonnage and truck cadence per pad.
  • Incumbent structure: last-mile software is bundled with proppant, containers, or trucking (Solaris, PropX/Liberty, Atlas, Vorto) — none is incented to sell the operator a neutral planning tool.
  • AI-assisted development lets a practitioner build the internal tool their vendor never would — FleetSync is the existence proof.

Market gap

Every serious software player in frac sand logistics is a hardware or services company first: their software exists to sell and utilize their assets, and it optimizes their slice — container cycling, silo inventory, truck dispatch. The operator's cross-fleet planning problem (multi-pad Gantt, hauler allocation, what-if scenarios, baseline-vs-actual) is nobody's product, because a neutral planning tool doesn't move sand tonnage or truck hours. So it defaults to Excel — unversioned, single-user, formula-fragile — at exactly the moment simul-frac pace and LNG-driven activity make the plan both more complex and more expensive to get wrong. The gap is the neutral, operator-owned planning layer; FleetSync is that layer, already shaped by production use inside a shale gas operator's completions program.
